  3. Creativity as a group phenomenon
    1. Processes
      1. Brainstorming (Osborn, 1957) is probably the most famous group process that is designed for improving creativity, but...
        1. It doesn't actually work! Real groups typically come up with HALF the ideas of "nominal groups" OR LESS! (Diehl & Stroebe, 1987)
        2. Possible sources of this difference include:
          • Production blocking, when people are busy listening instead of thinking, or have an idea and need to wait to share it until another group member finishes talking.
          • Evaluation apprehension, when people don't share ideas because they fear negative judgments of the other group members.
          • Free riding, where people do less work as group size increases because their contributions 1) are less identifiable and 2) have decreased perceived effectiveness.
    2. Leadership
      1. Leaders have different roles for supporting creativity in groups depending in the different phases of innovation. (Hunter & Cusherbery, 2011)
        1. Indirect leadership influences
          • Role modeling
          • Rewards and recognition
          • Hiring and team composition
          • Climate for creativity
        2. Direct leadership influences
          • Creative input and idea suggestion
          • Vision and strategy
          • Resource allocation
          • Decision making
